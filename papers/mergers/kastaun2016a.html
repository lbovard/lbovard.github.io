Structure of stable binary neutron star merger remnants: A case study

arxiv link - published in PRD

This paper studies the remanant of a BNS merger and analyses the properties of the remanant.

This paper uses the SHT EOS and has initial masses of 1.4 $M_{\odot}$, uses the WhiskyThermal code, no magnetic fields, and no neutrino transport.

To study the deformations, they use a different coordinate system in an attempt to remove the gauge-dependent effects.

Another point they address is the use of an alternative way for defining the bulk. Because defining a radius is difficult in GR, they propose using a coordinate independent definition of the mass by looking at surfaces of constant baryon mass density. In integral forms these are

$$ V(\rho') = \int_{\rho'}^{\infty} \int_{A(\rho)}dV,\\ M_{b}(\rho') = \int_{\rho'}^{\infty} \int_{A(\rho)}W\rho dV,$$

where $dV$ is the proper 3-volume, $A(\rho)$ is the proper isodensity surface of rest-mass density, $W$ is the Lorentz factor. To evaluate these integrals, they do the following. They take all the cells - ensuring that different refinement levels are properly considered - and then make a cumulative histogram with the density versus total mass. So to compute the mass, a given density is given and all values above that density are counted to give the mass. Likewise, once a density has been selected, the volume is easy to compute the same way. Thus for a given density, the mass and volume are given. From here they define the volumetric radius as $R_{V} = 4\pi R_{V}^{3}/3$. From here they define the compactness as $C_{V} = M_{b} / R_{v}$ which will be a function of density. To define the bulk, they simply select the maximum of this value.
