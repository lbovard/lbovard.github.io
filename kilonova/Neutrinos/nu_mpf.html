Neutrino Mean Free Path in Neutron Stars¶

\[\newcommand{\nue}{\nu_{e}} \newcommand{\nuae}{\bar{\nu}_{e}} \newcommand{\Ye}{Y_\mathrm{e}} \newcommand{\ms}{\,{\rm ms}} \newcommand{\km}{\,{\mathrm{km}}} \newcommand{\MeV}{\,{\mathrm{MeV}}} \newcommand{\gcm}{\,{\mathrm{g}/\mathrm{cm}^{3}}} \newcommand{\Msol}{\, M_{\odot}} \newcommand{\kB}{\, \rm{k_{B}/baryon}}\]

The mean free path \(\lambda\) is the average distance a particle must move before it interacts with another particle. The definition of the mean free path is

\[\lambda = \frac{1}{\sigma n},\]

where \(\sigma\) is the cross section in \(L^{2}\) and \(n\) is the number density in \(L^{-3}\) where \(L\) is the length scale. We are interested in the mean free path in the context of neutrino calculations as it tells us roughly where the neutrinos are free-streaming, i.e., with \(\lambda > 10\km\), versus trapped, i.e., with \(\lambda < 10 \km\). A value of \(10\km\) is chosen as the threshold as it is roughly the radius of the neutron star and represents a natural length scale in the simulations.

In order to calculate the \(\lambda\) of the neutrinos, we need to two pieces of information, the cross section \(\sigma\) and the number density \(n\). From above we can see that for all scattering processes the following relationship holds

\[\sigma \sim \sigma_{0}\left(\frac{E_{\nu}}{m_{e}c^{2}}\right)^{2},\]

where \(\sigma_{0}\approx 1.705\times 10^{-44}\,\textrm{cm}^{2}\) is the neutrino cross section and \(m_{e}c^{2} \approx 0.511 \MeV\) is the mass of the electron. Likewise, the number density is defined as

\[n = \frac{\rho}{n_{b}},\]

where \(n_{b} \approx 1.67\times 10^{-24}\, \textrm{g}\) is the baryonic mass, which we take to be simply the mass of the proton. Using the two relations, we can easily derive the expression for the mean free path

\[\lambda_{\nu} \sim 2.56\times 10^{14} \left(\frac{\rho}{1\gcm}\right)^{-1}\left(\frac{E_{\nu}}{1\MeV}\right)^{-2}\, \km.\]
import numpy as np
## set c = 1
#mass of the electron
me=0.5109989461 #MeV

#mass of the proton, which we take to be
#the baryonic mass. okay for rough calcs
mb=1.672621898e-24 #grams

#cross section of the neutrino
sigma0 = 1.705e-44 #cm^2

C=me**2 * mb / sigma0 #cm
C/=1e5 #convert to km


def free_path(logrho,logE):
  ''' the mean free path in km'''
  return (np.log10(C)-2*logE-logrho)

E=np.linspace(-2,2,100) #min 0.01 MeV , max 100 MeV
rho=np.linspace(2,16,100)

print "rho = {:1.2e} g/cm^3 E = {:2.2f} MeV l = {:.2f} km".format(1e14,1e1,10**free_path(14,1))
print "rho = {:1.2e} g/cm^3 E = {:2.2f} MeV l = {:.2f} km".format(1e11,np.log10(5),10**free_path(11,np.log10(5)))

rho = 1.00e+14 g/cm^3 E = 10.00 MeV l = 0.03 km
rho = 1.00e+11 g/cm^3 E = 0.70 MeV l = 102.46 km

The above shows some typical energies and temperatures reached inside a neutron star merger. The complete given picture is given as

In the above we plot this mean free path for the densities and neutrino energies encountered in neutron star mergers. The white line is the typical radius of a neutron star of \(10\km\) and the dot represents the typical values of the density, \(\rho_{\rm{NS}}\sim 10^{14}\gcm\), and the neutrino energy, \(E_{\nu}\sim 10\MeV\) encountered in the core of the neutron star. With these values, we get a typical mean free path of \(\lambda_{\rm{NS}}\sim 0.026\km\) in the core of the neutron star which is significantly smaller than that of the neutron star radius, and hence the term trapped. In contrast, if we consider typical values around \(\sim 50\km\) away from the centre, we have \(\rho\sim 10^{11}\gcm\) and \(E_{\nu}\sim 5 \MeV\) which gives \(\lambda \sim 100\km\). As this value is significantly larger than the neutron star, the neutrinos are considered to be free-streaming. The transition from \(10^{14}\gcm\) to \(10^{11}\gcm\), i.e., from trapped to free-streaming, in neutron star mergers occurs over a length of \(\sim 10\km\) which makes implementations of neutrino schemes interesting as they need to be able hand this transition accurately.
